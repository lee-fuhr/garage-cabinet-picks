How to choose a garage cabinets

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

To choose the right metal garage storage cabinets with doors, start by measuring your space and deciding what you’ll store. Look for cabinets made from heavy-gauge steel (16- or 18-gauge is best), with a durable powder-coat finish, adjustable shelves, and locking doors if security matters. Prioritize brands known for quality like Gladiator, NewAge, or Husky, and always consider assembly difficulty and weight capacity per shelf.

What material should you look for in garage cabinets?

Metal cabinets are the standard for garages because they resist moisture, pests, and dents better than wood or particleboard. The key is the gauge of steel. Thicker steel, 16-gauge or 18-gauge, means the shelves and frame won’t buckle under heavy loads. Cabinets made from thinner steel (22-gauge or higher) may feel flimsy and are prone to bending when you load them with toolboxes or automotive fluids. Look for a powder-coat finish rather than painted metal. Powder coating is baked on and holds up better against scratches, humidity, and temperature swings. Some cabinets come with a textured finish that hides fingerprints and smudges. Avoid bare steel or low-quality paint that can chip or rust, especially in uninsulated garages with high humidity.

How much weight do garage cabinets need to hold?

Weight capacity is one of the most important factors. A well-built metal cabinet should have shelves rated for at least 75 to 100 pounds per shelf. For heavy items like power tools, cast-iron vises, or stacked storage bins, look for cabinets that advertise a per-shelf capacity of 150 pounds or more. The total cabinet weight rating, including the top, matters if you plan to set a large tool chest or air compressor on top. Keep in mind that adjustable shelves usually have lower capacity than fixed shelves because the clips or brackets bear the load. Some cabinets include metal cross-braces or welded supports underneath shelves to prevent sagging. If you’re storing automotive parts or heavy steel hardware, prioritize cabinets with reinforced shelves rather than those with simple wire or peg-style support.

What size and configuration fits your garage?

Before you buy, measure the wall space you plan to use. Standard single-door cabinets are about 18 to 24 inches wide, but you can find narrower units (12 inches) for tight spots or wider units (36 to 48 inches) with double doors. Depth is typically between 12 and 24 inches; deeper cabinets hold bulkier items but stick out farther, which can interfere with opening car doors. Height usually ranges from 36 inches (counter-height) to 72 inches (tall floor cabinets) or full-length wall cabinets. Consider stacking or modular systems. Brands like Gladiator and NewAge offer interlocking cabinets that can be arranged side by side or stacked. Tall cabinets with multiple shelves maximize vertical storage for items like spray cans, hardware, or cleaning supplies. For functional work space, a counter-height cabinet (around 36 inches tall) with a sturdy top can double as a workbench. Always check the unit’s footprint and whether it requires a wall anchor or floor mounting for stability.

Do you need adjustable shelves?

Adjustable shelves are a huge plus because they let you reconfigure the interior as your storage needs change. Many metal cabinets have shelves on clips that can be moved up or down in small increments. Some use peg-style support with multiple holes every two inches. Adjustable shelves make it easy to store tall bottles, large tool cases, or odd-shaped equipment without wasted space. However, not all adjustable systems are equal. Better cabinets have thicker shelf edges and metal clips that lock securely rather than simple plastic supports. Check whether the shelves themselves are aluminum or painted metal, solid steel shelves with a rolled edge resist bending better than thin stamped shelves. If you rarely change your storage layout, fixed-shelf cabinets may be simpler and often cost less, but you sacrifice future flexibility.

Locking vs. non-locking doors – what’s right for you?

Locking doors add security for valuable tools, hazardous chemicals, or items you want to keep away from children. Good cabinets use a keyed lock, often with a central locking rod that engages top and bottom. Some high-end models offer a padlock tang or a combination lock. If you’re storing expensive power tools or firearms (where legal), a lock is essential. But locks add cost and can be a nuisance if you access the contents frequently. For general garage storage, yard gear, sports equipment, cleaning supplies, non-locking cabinets with magnetic catches or friction latches work fine. Magnetic catches are convenient; mechanical latches can wear out or jam. Also consider that locking cabinets often require a special blank key replacement, so keep a spare key somewhere safe. If security isn’t a concern, you can put the money toward heavier-duty shelves or a better finish.

Assembly and installation – what to expect?

Most metal garage cabinets come flat-packed and require assembly, even from premium brands. Expect to spend one to three hours per cabinet depending on complexity and whether you’re working alone. Hardware is usually provided, but a power drill with a hex bit and a socket wrench will speed things up. The biggest complaint from owners is poorly written instructions or stripped rivet holes. Choose brands with a reputation for clear assembly guides and consistent quality control. Installation often requires anchoring to the wall for safety, especially for tall or top-heavy cabinets. Most units include L-brackets or a tip-over restraint. Use concrete anchors on masonry walls or substantial toggle bolts on drywall into studs. Floor-mounting is rarely needed for well-built cabinets unless you live in an earthquake-prone area. If you plan a modular setup, measure level lines and check for any warping before bolting units together. Proper leveling prevents doors from sagging and keeps shelves aligned.

Frequently asked questions

Are steel garage cabinets better than wood?

Generally yes for a garage environment. Steel does not warp, rot, or attract termites like wood. It’s also easier to clean if you spill oil or paint. Wood cabinets can look nicer but require sealing and may swell in humid garages. Metal is more durable and easier to maintain long-term.

Can I put heavy tools on top of a metal cabinet?

It depends on the cabinet’s top weight rating. Many cabinets have a reinforced top that can hold up to 50–100 pounds. Check the manufacturer’s specs. Never exceed the rated load, and always distribute weight evenly. Heavy items like bench grinders or parts washers are best placed on a dedicated workbench.

How do I secure cabinets to the wall?

Most cabinets include pre-drilled holes and a tip-over restraint bracket. Use concrete anchors for masonry walls or heavy-duty toggle bolts into wood studs for drywall. Driving screws directly into drywall without anchors is not safe. Ensure the cabinet is level before anchoring to avoid door alignment issues.

What is the best brand for metal garage cabinets?

Gladiator and NewAge are widely respected for heavy-duty construction and modular flexibility. Husky from Home Depot offers solid mid-range options, and Homak provides budget-friendly choices. Avoid no-name brands with very thin steel and vague specs. Look for brands with extensive owner feedback and available replacement parts.

Do I need a vented or closed-back cabinet for a garage?

Closed-back cabinets keep dust and spiders out of stored items and look cleaner. Vented cabinets allow air circulation, which can reduce moisture buildup if you store damp tools or garden supplies. In humid climates, a closed-back with a good seal may still trap moisture; consider adding a desiccant pack. For most general garage storage, closed-back is preferred.

Can metal cabinets rust in an unheated garage?

They can if the finish is compromised and humidity is high. Powder-coated steel is very resistant but not immune. To prevent rust, keep cabinets away from direct ground contact (use a riser or pallet), avoid storing wet items inside, and touch up any scratches with matching paint. Good ventilation in the garage also helps.

In shortChoosing metal garage cabinets with doors comes down to matching the steel gauge, weight capacity, and configuration to the tools and gear you actually own. Invest in cabinets with thick steel, a quality powder-coat finish, and adjustable shelves for long-term flexibility. Secure tall units to the wall, and don’t overlook the difference a locking door makes if security matters. A little planning now will save you from sagging shelves, rust spots, and wasted space later.
